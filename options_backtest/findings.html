A plain-English scoreboard of every options strategy tested: iron condors, "Zen" credit spreads, and a pile of overnight experiments. No jargon, just results.

📖 Read this first (30 seconds)

Each strategy below is a way of selling options credit spreads on the NIFTY index — you collect a premium up front and hope the market behaves so you keep it. I tested 24 different versions on roughly the last 12–13 months of real market data (about a 390-day window).

Two families made money: iron condors (bet the market stays in a range) and held-to-expiry directional spreads (the "Zen" style). A big batch of overnight-hold ideas all lost money. The numbers below are backtests — see the reality check at the bottom before you get excited.

⭐The stand-outs

The four strategies worth actually thinking about — a mix of best return and best consistency.

🎯Iron Condors — "the market stays in a range"

You sell a call spread and a put spread at the same time. You win as long as the index doesn't move too far in either direction — no need to guess up or down.

👍 Pros

  • No direction call needed — it profits from a calm, range-bound market.
  • The best config returned +57%, and several landed in the steady +18% to +37% band.
  • Risk is capped on both sides by the protective "wings", so no single trade can blow up the account.

👎 Cons

  • Deep dips. Even the good ones dropped 14–29% along the way — you'd need a strong stomach.
  • The top-return version (tight 1% strikes) has a huge average loss — one trending month can erase many small wins.
  • Fussy. Widen the strikes too far (2.5%) or only trade in "calm" markets and it flips to a loss.

🧘"Zen" Directional Spreads — held to expiry

Pick a direction using a simple signal, sell a one-sided credit spread a couple of days before expiry, and hold it to the finish. "Zen" = set it and let time do the work.

👍 Pros

  • The two signal-driven ones (Momentum & Mean-Reversion) were the most consistent of everything tested.
  • Mean-Reversion had the calmest ride — smallest drawdown (−3%) and by far the best win-to-loss ratio.
  • Simple to run: enter near expiry, hold, done.

👎 Cons

  • Mean-Reversion only took 18 trades — a small sample that could partly be luck.
  • You need a real signal: the "no-signal" benchmark that just sells blindly lost money.
  • Holding to expiry means you eat the full move if the market goes against you late.

❌Overnight Experiments — the dead end

Nine variations that opened a spread and held it just overnight, based on different signals. Every single one lost money — shown here so we don't retry them.

👎 Why they all failed

  • Every variant lost, from −15% to −71%. This isn't one bad idea — the whole approach is weak.
  • Holding option spreads overnight means price gaps and time-decay quirks work against you here.
  • More trades didn't help — the biggest losers also traded the most, just bleeding faster.

🔎 The one takeaway

  • They were cheap and fast to test, which is exactly why we ran them.
  • Clear verdict: overnight holding is off the table — the edge lives in holding to expiry, not overnight.

⚠️Reality check — please read before trusting any number above

  • These are backtests on ~13 months of history, not live results. Past behaviour is not a promise.
  • The edges shrink once real trading costs and slippage are subtracted — the thin winners can get much thinner.
  • Small samples lie. Mean-Reversion (18 trades) and the low-vol condor (30 trades) don't have enough history to fully trust.
  • A high win rate ≠ safe. The no-signal benchmark won ~79% of trades and still lost money because a couple of losses were huge.
  • Sensible next step: run the top 1–2 strategies as a small live paper-trade for a few weeks before risking real capital — not another round of tweaking the backtest.

📚Tiny glossary

Return %
How much the account grew (or shrank) over the ~13-month test window.
Win rate
The share of trades that ended in a profit. High win rate is nice but not the whole story.
Max drawdown
The worst peak-to-bottom dip along the way. −20% means the account was once 20% below its high point. Lower is calmer.
Profit factor
Total winnings ÷ total losses. Above 1.0 = profitable; 2.0 means it won ₹2 for every ₹1 lost.
Credit spread
You sell one option and buy a cheaper one for protection, pocketing the difference as your max profit.
Iron condor
Two credit spreads at once (one above, one below the market). Profits if the market stays in the middle.
