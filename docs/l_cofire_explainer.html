Backend change · 2026‑07‑12

What we changed, and why it matters

A plain-English walkthrough of the "Method L" update to the breakout scanner's conviction score — written for someone who doesn't (yet) know a base depth from a breakout.

TL;DR — The scanner already ranks stocks with a single 0–100 conviction score built from three ingredients: (1) how reliably a stock's past breakouts followed through, (2) how deep its price base was, and (3) whether other independent breakout signals fired on the same day ("confirmation"). We tested four new candidate confirmation signals (nicknamed I, J, K, L) to see if any of them predicted better follow-through. Three didn't hold up statistically and were discarded. One — "Method L", a deep-base + volatility-squeeze pattern — did (a real, statistically significant lift), so it's now wired into the conviction score as a third confirmation input. Nothing about the screen's layout changed — no new badge or column — only the ranking math for a subset of stocks got slightly smarter, and only in the India market (the US side ignores confirmation signals entirely, for reasons explained below).

1

Stock-market beginner primer

Before the "before vs after," here's what the scanner is even looking at. Six ideas cover almost everything below.

📈

Breakout

A stock pushes above a price ceiling ("resistance") it had struggled to clear before — often on above-average volume. The bet: if it clears the ceiling convincingly, it tends to keep climbing.

🧱

Base

The sideways, range-bound period before a breakout — price "resting" under resistance while buyers and sellers fight it out. A deep base (a bigger up‑and‑down range beforehand) has historically led to stronger breakouts than a shallow, tight one.

🚧

Resistance

A price level a stock has bounced off before — think of it as a ceiling. Breaking through it is the whole premise of a "breakout" trade.

✅

Follow-through

Did the breakout actually keep working afterward, or did it fizzle/reverse? This is the thing we're ultimately trying to predict — measured here as "did it move +1R (a defined risk unit) before hitting its stop, within a set window."

🎯

Conviction score (0–100)

One number the app ranks every stock by. Higher = "this setup is both close to firing and has a track record / features that historically follow through." It is not a probability — it's a ranking.

🤝

Confirmation / "co-fire"

The scanner runs several independent breakout-detection methods (nicknamed A, D, E2, L…) side by side. When the main method (A) breaks out and a second, independent method agrees on the very same day, that agreement is called a "co-fire" — like getting a second doctor's opinion that matches the first.

2

How the conviction score worked before this change

Nothing about breakout detection itself changed — a stock still needs to actually break out (Method A) to show up as a candidate. What changed is the quality/ranking math layered on top, called breakout_quality(). It already blended three validated ingredients:

60%

① Track-record reliability

Out of this stock's own past breakouts, how many actually followed through? A stock with zero history isn't punished (it starts at the market's overall average, ~39% in India), and one single failed breakout barely moves the needle — you need several data points before the score gets genuinely cautious.

25%

② Base depth

How deep was the price base beforehand? Backtesting found deeper bases break out more reliably than shallow, tight ones — the opposite of what many beginners assume. An 8%-deep base scores 0; a 50%+-deep base scores close to 1.

15%

③ Method confirmation

Did another independent breakout method also fire today? Before this change, only two were counted: "E2" (a relative-strength breakout vs. the market) worth half credit, and "D" worth the other half. Full credit only if both fired.

Why these three, and not more? Every ingredient here had to survive a statistical test across thousands of historical breakouts before being allowed into the formula — see section 6 for the long list of things that were tried and rejected (chart patterns, volume spikes, ADX, etc.).
quality = 0.60 × reliability + 0.25 × base_depth + 0.15 × method_confirmation where, before this change: method_confirmation = (0.5 if E2 fired today else 0) + (0.5 if D fired today else 0)
3

The experiment: could we find a better third opinion?

The scanner already runs several other pattern-detection methods behind the scenes that weren't being used for confirmation yet. Four candidates were tested — did stocks that had one of these fire on the same day as the main breakout follow through more often than stocks that didn't? Tested across 9,581 historical whole-market breakout events.

NicknameWhat it detects (plain English)Sample sizeFollow-through liftStatistically real?
I Price sitting on a high-volume "shelf" from recent trading (volume-profile support) 2,705 events +1.6 pts ✕ No (p = 0.15)
J A volatility "squeeze" per the TTM-Squeeze indicator (price coiling tightly) 2,386 events −0.5 pts ✕ No (p = 0.68)
K Price anchored above a volume-weighted average price (VWAP) drawn from a recent swing low 667 events +2.8 pts ✕ No (p = 0.15)
L ★ Deep price base + volatility squeeze releasing into a breakout, in one combined pattern 1,637 events +6.5 pts (44.4% vs 37.9%) ✓ Yes (p < 0.001)
What does "p-value" mean here, in beginner terms? It's the odds this "lift" was just random noise rather than a real pattern. A common bar in research is p < 0.05 ("less than 5% chance it's a fluke"). I, J, and K all landed around p ≈ 0.15–0.68 — i.e., a coin flip could plausibly explain their results, so they were not reliable enough to trust with real rankings. L landed at p < 0.001 — under a 1-in-1000 chance of being a fluke.
Discarding I, J, and K wasn't a failure — it's exactly the point of testing first. Shipping a signal that looks plausible but doesn't actually predict anything would make the scanner less trustworthy, not more (it'd just be adding noise dressed up as insight).
4

What we added: Method L joins the formula

Method L now counts as a third, partial-credit confirmation signal — worth less than E2/D individually (since it's newer and slightly less powerful), but real.

method_confirmation = (0.5 if E2 fired) + (0.5 if D fired) + (0.25 if L fired) — capped at 1.0 total quality formula itself is unchanged — L just feeds into ingredient ③: quality = 0.60 × reliability + 0.25 × base_depth + 0.15 × method_confirmation
Net effect, measured on the full 9,581-event replay: the gap in follow-through rate between the scanner's best-ranked third of setups and its worst-ranked third widened from +6.1 points to +7.1 points — i.e., the ranking got measurably better at separating good setups from bad ones, without breaking anything that already worked.

So Method L firing has zero effect on US conviction scores today — it's wired in, but structurally inert until/unless a future US-specific study justifies turning it on.

6

What we deliberately did not add (and why)

For context, here's the fuller list of things that have been tested against real historical data over time and rejected from the score because they didn't hold up, or actively hurt. This is why the conviction score can be trusted to mean something — everything in it survived a test, and everything here didn't:

✕ ADX (a trend-strength indicator) — not predictive in either market.
✕ Volume-surge magnitude — how big the volume spike was didn't matter, just whether it happened at all.
✕ Named chart patterns (cup-and-handle, flags, etc.) — visually appealing, not statistically predictive.
✕ Volatility contraction — flipped direction between markets; an artifact, not a real signal.
✕ Broad market regime (index above/below its 200-day average) — tested, no measurable edge.
✕ "Closest historical analog day" — still shown to users as a soft illustration, but too weak/noisy to feed the ranking math.

Glossary — every term used above, in one place

Method AThe scanner's primary/baseline breakout definition — price clears resistance in an uptrend. This is what actually puts a stock "on the list" in the first place.
Method D, E2, L…Alternate, independently-defined breakout/pattern detectors running in parallel to Method A. They don't add new stocks to the list on their own here — they're used as "does this agree with Method A today?" confirmation checks.
Co-fire / confirmationTwo different detection methods both flagging the same stock on the same day. Historically, agreement between independent methods has predicted stronger follow-through than Method A firing alone.
Reliability estimateA stock's own personal breakout track record, statistically smoothed ("Bayesian shrinkage") so that one unlucky failed breakout doesn't unfairly tank its score — it needs a real pattern of failures (3+) before the score gets meaningfully cautious.
Base depthHow big the price swing was during the sideways "resting" period before the breakout. Counterintuitively, deeper (bigger-range) bases have back-tested as more reliable than shallow ones.
ImminenceHow close/urgent the setup is right now (e.g. "breaking out today" vs. "just starting to coil"). The conviction score blends this with quality — a great-quality setup that's not near triggering yet still ranks below one that's actually breaking today.
p-valueA statistics term for "how likely is it this result happened by pure chance?" Smaller is stronger evidence of a real effect. p < 0.05 is a common minimum bar; this study used p < 0.001 for Method L.
BacktestRunning a rule against years of historical price data to see how it would have performed, before trusting it with real decisions today.
